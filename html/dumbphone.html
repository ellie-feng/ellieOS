dear diary,

my digital minimalism + dumbphone journey

today i realised that my dumbphone did NOT cure my smartphone addiction

acknowledging the problem

I started noticing how often I reached for my phone when I was bored, anxious, or uncomfortable. Reading *Digital Minimalism* made me realise that this wasn’t just a bad habit—this was behavioural addiction shaped by devices designed to keep us scrolling.

solution 1: the 'dumb' smartphone

I tried stripping my iPhone down to its bare essentials—no social media, no distractions, just calls, messages, and a few practical apps. It helped, but I still found myself opening Safari to access the web versions of the apps I deleted. My brain was still craving the dopamine.

solution 2: the dumbphone

So I went further. I bought a Nokia 3210 4G. Messaging was slower, everything required intention, and after a few days, I started feeling genuinely at peace. But the addiction didn’t vanish—I simply moved the scrolling to my laptop and iPad.

solution 3: the decentralised approach

I separated all the things my phone used to do into individual devices: a dedicated music device, an old Canon Powershot camera, a Kobo ereader, etc. By removing capabilities from my phone, I removed opportunities to scroll.

you don't need a dumbphone to be a digital minimalist

Small steps help: delete non-essential apps, turn off notifications, switch to greyscale, and try analogue tools for everyday tasks.

reflection

This journey isn’t about replacing your phone—it’s about rebuilding your relationship with attention and intention. Progress isn’t linear, but resilience matters more than perfection.

- ellie 21.11.24
